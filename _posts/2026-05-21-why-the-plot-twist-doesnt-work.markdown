**WARNING**: This blog post contains **SPOILERS** for the game, *Detroit: Become Human* (DBH). Please play the game first before reading! (Or don't, it's entirely up to you.)


I've seen the topic surrounding the "twist" that Alice is an android and not a human be brought up time and time again, especially on the Detroit: Become Human subreddit. And honestly, I genuinely believe that the twist *does not work.*

I know there are a lot of people in support of the twist, and a lot of the arguments suggest that it is supposed to be a test for a player; to see whether they still view Alice as a person regardless, or whether it changes the player’s view on her and cause them to abandon her.

And to be fair, I *absolutely* understand and appreciate that that’s what the game was trying to do.

**Key word: trying.**

The way I see it, DBH is still a video game at the end of the day. Regardless of whether its gameplay fits that of a typical video game, or whether it plays more as an interactive movie than a video game, it’s still a *video game*, where you are playing in the perspective of a specific character. When developing video games where you are playing as a certain character, it is very important to ensure that the player’s motives, decisions, feelings, etc. align with the character’s, and vice versa, as the character is, ultimately, an extension of the player.

Now, I’m not saying that every single character in video games needs to be a self-insert per se – it never needs to go to that extreme – but if there is any mismatch in common aspects such as motives between the player and the character, it is going to break immersion for the player, and cause the game to feel clunky as a result.

And this twist is a prime example of that; if this twist was supposed to be a test for the *player*, it should also be a test for the *character*.

Now people can argue that it is also a test for the character, since she always ignored the fact that Alice was an android and would refuse to accept her as such. However:

1. There is really no obvious proof that she was ignoring this fact prior to Crossroads (I will elaborate shortly on this), and, more importantly;

2. There isn’t any real (believable) motive to her wanting to view Alice as a human like there is for the player, or any in-game explanation as to why she wants to (I'll elaborate on this as well), especially since she can literally say this to Markus (when talking to him about Alice) right before the twist:

To elaborate more on my first point, there are many “hints” towards the twist that people have insisted make it "obvious," even though they aren’t necessarily obvious for everyone on a first playthrough; this ultimately results in this player/character “mismatch” that I was discussing earlier (and as briefly mentioned too, I'm not really listing these items as a way to support the twist or suggest that it was well-written, but more so rather to highlight that it wasn’t really obvious to everyone (or, at least, wasn't obvious to *me*) that she was denying Alice being an android).

Some of these have included the following:

1. The magazine in A New Home. I've seen a lot of people say that this was supposed to be the “most obvious” hint, and an absolute “dead giveaway” of the twist, even though the magazine was *intentionally* blurred/obscured until the legible, non-blurred version was shown during Crossroads. Stating that this hint was as obvious as it was would be no different than telling a heavily nearsighted person how "obvious" it is that there is a bird in a tree 50 feet away, or telling a colourblind person how "obvious" it is that an object is red.
       
2. There’s also the argument that Alice never ate, or always refused eating, which further “confirmed” that she was an android. This is also an argument that I hesitate getting behind, because just because we didn’t specifically see her eat *on screen* doesn’t necessarily mean she wasn’t eating *at all*. Theoretically, by that logic, characters such as Kamski, Leo, Ben, Chris, Perkins, etc. who also were never shown eating on screen could also be androids, even though it’s very well established that they aren’t.

*(Now I have heard some headcanons/theories about Kamski uploading his consciousness into an android body and no longer being human, but that’s an entirely different story altogether that isn’t mentioned in canon and that I won’t get into here.)*

And I hate to say it too, but to assume a character just doesn’t eat *at all* just because we don’t see them eat on screen is... superficial if that’s the right word? Not to mention all of the other plausible reasons why she could have been refusing to eat assuming she was human: For instance, she probably refused to eat Rose’s spaghetti in Midnight Train because the events of Stormy Night made spaghetti triggering for her; she also could have been refusing the chocolate bar you steal in Fugitives because she didn’t want to eat something that was stolen (which it is shown during that chapter that she is very heavily against Kara stealing from the convenience store, so this would also make sense). Not to mention too, Kara’s one comment in The Pirates’ Cove chapter about how there are “cookies left” in Luther’s bag could further imply that Alice may have eaten some of the cookies already while they were in the car on the way to the fun fair/Rose’s house:

{% include embed-audio.html src="/assets/audio/X0701K_SHOP_COOKIES_PC_X07KKARA_LIKESOME_ENG.wav" %}

There are a lot of reasons why she could have been refusing to eat on screen, and a lot of opportunities off screen where she could have potentially eaten. And especially considering that Kara was being “delusional” because of these “missed queues” according to the fanbase, it is hard for the player to also feel like they’re being “delusional” alongside the character, or see Kara clearly being delusional, because to the player, there are *actual legitimate reasons/conclusions* to why Alice behaves the way she does. This “mismatch” ultimately comes from Kara truly being “delusional” and *actively ignoring* that Alice is an android, and the player *actually assuming* Alice as human based on the evidence given.
	
And honestly, I wouldn’t even say they did a good job writing Kara as delusional. And	they obviously can write delusional characters, because Connor is proof of that. Connor is very clearly delusional to the player and is clearly written as such.
       
3. Also a bit of an off topic “bonus” about Alice’s one quote in Midnight Train and her use of the word “us”:

{% include embed-audio.html src="/assets/audio/X0801K_ALICE_TAKECARE_PC_X08KALICE_01CONT_ENG.wav" %}

If I remember correctly, this was the only “hint” that I really caught on my first playthrough; and I put the word “hint” in quotes because when I originally heard it, I didn’t interpret it in the literal sense of, “Oh, she’s referring to herself as one of them, that must mean she’s an android!” – which would ultimately hint towards the twist – but rather in the metaphorical sense of, “Oh she’s referring to herself as one of them, because she’s been able to see the entire ‘Android vs. Human’ stance from an android’s perspective from essentially having android parents for a week, and doesn’t see any difference between herself and them as they are all just people at the end of the day.” And honestly to me, viewing this quote from this metaphorical point of view, while still under the assumption that Alice is human, actually would have made this one of the most significant quotes in the game, and it actually would have emphasized and honed in more on the message that the twist was actually trying to convey than the twist itself had.

One of the main pieces of evidence that I have heard suggesting why Kara actually wanted to view Alice as a human was because (and hopefully I get this right) if she did, then she wasn’t fully deviating, since she was programmed to be a mother to human children and not android children, and then the twist in Crossroads was her fully accepting Alice as an android, and thus fully deviating from her programming. What confuses me about this argument, is that it implies that Kara doesn't *want* to fully deviate in Stormy Night, or hesitates deviating, even though she has no implied motive for not wanting to. At least with Connor, he literally fears even so much as *expressing emotion* (and wrongly convincing himself that he *can’t*) because of the potential that Cyberlife can completely shut him and his model line down if he deviates, so it makes a lot of sense why *he* would hesitate deviating. As well, if anything, I would think she’d be *more* inclined to help Alice escape her situation if she was literally the same species as her, more so than if she was a different species that was especially known to *harm* hers.

And at least the player actually had real evidence to initially assume Alice was, and to treat her as, human, early on in the game (her having a last name unlike most other androids in canon is an example of this), and also not to mention how much more dynamic it could have made both Kara and Alice’s relationship and narrative. The reason why people were invested in their story initially was because of this type of relationship, and how an android parent could raise a human child, how a human child could be raised by an android parent, how to write a human child that ages quicker than their android parent, etc. Now, I do agree that we have Markus and Carl, as well as Connor and Hank for these types of dynamic android/human relationships, and you could even theoretically argue that Kara and Rose’s relationship could be included into the mix. But my argument is that these relationships are sufficient for the player to recognise androids as people, and that an extra “plot twist” to try and reinforce this fact is therefore never needed, especially when Kara’s story is ultimately (tarnished is too strong of a word) hindered as a result.
